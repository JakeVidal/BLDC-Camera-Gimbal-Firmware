Problem Definition

What is a Gimbal?

A gimbal is a mechanical assembly with multiple axes of rotation, which allows an object placed at the centre to remain stationary as the frame moves freely. Gimbals find applications in many domains where stabilization is necessary.

Image capture, as an example, requires the compensation of vibration and movement to reduce image blurring during capture, and gimbals have often been used for this purpose.

Our Target Audience

UVic AERO competes in an annual competition for Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV’s), run by Unmanned Systems Canada. The competition awards more points for more custom solutions.

This project was undertaken because it will enable UVic AERO to mount complex payloads and undertake missions where real-time, high-resolution footage is critical.

Uvic AERO's Problem

The problem as formulated by AERO is to design the hardware and firmware of an electronic control system for an existing Brushless DC (BLDC) electric motor camera gimbal, whose capabilities and characteristics should include:

  • Stabilizing at the pitch and yaw of a payload consisting of a camera
  • Smoothing movement to eliminate jitter from the image acquisition system
  • Receiving and obeying pan and tilt commands at a distance
  • Controlling BLDC motor angular position to achieve stabilization and executing pan and tilt commands
  • Autonomous tracking of objects
  • Integrate into AERO’s existing infrastructure and ecosystem

Object Tracking Architecture

In 2016, AERO built a camera gimbal for image stabilization and capture. At the time, only the mechanical design was completed, and was based on brushless DC motors. An off-the-shelf hobbyist, closed-source hardware-firmware control solution was purchased. While the system worked, it lacked several desirable features, such as software- based control, object tracking and extensibility.

In 2017, a system failure prompted the exploration of pursuing an in-house control solution.

The project was undertaken because it will enable UVic AERO to mount complex payloads and undertake missions where real-time, high-resolution footage is critical. Past AERO missions would have benefited from this system – for example, solar farm damage monitoring.
